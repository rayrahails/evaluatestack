Web Development

4 Dev Teams. Zero Clarity on Who Could Actually Ship.

A mid-size SaaS company had four web development teams and a backlog of projects piling up. Leadership kept assigning work based on availability and seniority. Two projects had already missed their deadlines by 3+ weeks.

I mapped the capabilities of all four teams against the actual project requirements. Not just "frontend vs backend" but the specific skills each project demanded: API architecture, legacy code migration, client-facing delivery under pressure, cross-team dependency management.

The result? Three of the four teams had critical blind spots that nobody had identified. One team that was consistently overlooked for high-profile work turned out to be the strongest match for the company's most complex project. Within one quarter, on-time delivery improved by 35% and the two stalled projects shipped within weeks of reassignment.

Enterprise IT

$2M Infrastructure Project. Wrong Team Lead. Caught It Before Launch.

A financial services firm was about to assign their most senior engineer to lead a cloud migration project. On paper, perfect fit. 10 years of experience. Great track record.

I ran a capability evaluation across the project's actual requirements: vendor negotiation, cross-department stakeholder alignment, regulatory compliance documentation, and real-time incident escalation. The senior engineer scored exceptionally on technical execution but had significant gaps in stakeholder management and compliance documentation, two capabilities that this specific project depended on heavily.

They reassigned the lead role to a less senior but better-matched engineer and moved the original candidate to the technical architecture role where his strengths actually lived. The project launched on schedule and passed its first compliance audit without a single revision.
